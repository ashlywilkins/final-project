Why Are Orcas Unique

Pygmy killer whales are a small (maximum recorded length of 2.6 m) odontocete with a rounded head. The front half of the body is quite robust and then tapers to a narrow peduncle. The pectoral flippers are rounded at the tips and they have a relatively large dorsal fin. The relatively large dorsal fin in proportion to the amount of back visible when surfacing is perhaps the easiest way of distinguishing photographs of this species from false killer whales. Pygmy killer whales have white lips and in older animals this white may extend away from the mouth-line onto the chin and rostrum. They have a dark gray dorsal cape and are lighter gray laterally, and the demarcation between the dorsal and lateral pigmentation is quite distinctive, unlike the similar looking melon-headed whale. Individuals frequently have paired linear white scars from interacting with other pygmy killer whales, this too is one easy way of distinguishing them from melon-headed whales, which only infrequently have such scars. The teeth are very large relative to the size of the body, perhaps the largest for any species.
